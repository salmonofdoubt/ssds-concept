What this system actually does

The SSDS is designed as a decision model rather than a full process-response simulator. Its job is to show where NbS portfolios should be prioritised first, which portfolios are favoured, how recommendations behave when assumptions shift, and what minimum verification still needs to happen before action. The result is an interpretable claim, not just a colourful map.

1. Evidence architecture

Combine official assessment inputs, environmental spatial layers, protected-area constraints, and derived analytical variables into a governance-anchored evidence stack.

2. Rule-explicit decision logic

Formalise exclusions, eligibility rules, suitability rules, portfolio dependencies, do-no-harm constraints, and scaling assumptions into an inspectable rule sheet.

3. Stability-aware prioritisation

Run perturbation experiments across data, parameter, structural, and scale uncertainty to classify recommendations as robust, conditional, or fragile.

Interpretive distinction. Eligibility asks whether a measure can be considered. Suitability asks whether the environmental and pathway context fit. Priority asks where action should happen first. Stability asks whether that recommendation survives plausible perturbations. Maintainability then shapes sequencing and confidence for implementation.

Primary Doctoral Data Generation

The thesis generates georeferenced empirical evidence to ground-truth assumptions and calibrate rule stability.

Reach-Audit Variables

Georeferenced field observations of bank condition, livestock access, and visible connectivity used for misclassification diagnosis.

Empirical data on implementation persistence: repair burden, recurrent pressure, and visibility for inspection.

Repeat-audit data used to quantify observer and seasonal variance within the perturbation logic.

Ranking engine and algorithm spine

The SSDS is not a flat weighted-average machine. It combines exclusions, eligibility logic, lexicographic ranking, portfolio assignment, stability diagnostics, and sequencing rules. Maintainability affects implementation readiness rather than secretly altering the core environmental claim. 

for each decision_unit in spatial_frame:
    if exclusion_mask == TRUE:
        status = "impossible / excluded"
    else:
        eligibility = f(pathway_presence,
                        receiving_water_sensitivity,
                        land_cover_fit,
                        hydromorphology,
                        protected_area_constraints,
                        do_no_harm_rules)
        if eligibility < threshold:
            status = "eligible but low plausibility"
        else:
            status = "send to ranking engine"
This stage separates impossible, weak, and genuinely plausible intervention space.

What flows into eligibility

  • Governance layers. WFD/RBMP context, Area for Action, protected sites, catchment restoration priorities.
  • Pathway fit. Source-pathway-receptor logic, terrain, corridor opportunity, wetness, connectivity.
  • Constraint handling. Exclusions, do-no-harm rules, ecological safeguards.
rank candidate units by ordered objective logic:

1. maximise nutrient_reduction_tier
2. break ties using biodiversity_connectivity_tier
3. break remaining ties using flood_attenuation_tier
4. break remaining ties using sediment_reduction_tier
5. retain rule trace, assumptions, and evidence-quality flags

output:
    eligible / low-priority / strategic-priority surfaces
    with explicit confidence and rationale
The core point is hierarchy. Secondary objectives refine the decision. They do not silently override the nutrients-first mission.

Why lexicographic ordering matters

  • Nutrient reduction stays primary. This keeps water-quality delivery at the centre of the model.
  • Biodiversity is operationalised as connectivity. Corridor continuity and ecological safeguards refine placement.
  • Flood and sediment remain structured secondary objectives. They improve portfolio coherence without flattening priorities into one composite score.

Riparian corridor

Buffers, fencing, hedgerow or native planting, and corridor continuity enhancement.

Runoff interception

Field-edge strips, swales, sediment traps, and small attenuation features.

Wetland package

Treatment wetland plus upstream interception logic and downstream verification triggers.

Floodplain package

Floodplain reconnection or wet grassland restoration combined with habitat logic.

Peat / organic-soil protection

Avoidance, constraint handling, or rewetting-oriented logic where relevant.

Portfolio principle

Interacting measures are treated as systems. One intervention often depends on whether another is also present.

sequencing_score = g(priority_rank,
                     portfolio_coherence,
                     stability_class,
                     evidence_quality,
                     practical_implementation_logic,
                     maintainability)

if stability_class == "robust" and maintainability high:
    phase = "early action"
elif stability_class == "conditional":
    phase = "targeted verification / conservative deployment"
else:
    phase = "do not claim early confidence"
Maintainability is explicit here. A reach can look attractive on a map and still be a poor early-action candidate if persistence is weak.

Maintainability variables

  • Recurrent stock pressure. Is the corridor likely to be damaged again?
  • Access and inspection visibility. Can the intervention be checked and maintained in practice?
  • Repair burden and conflict risk. Drain-cleaning conflict, mowing conflict, bank-instability burden.
  • Persistence without repeated intervention. Durable fit matters more than apparent fit.

Perturbation ingredients in the actual proposal

  • Data. Missingness, classification error, vintage changes, resolution changes.
  • Parameter. Thresholds, breakpoints, connectivity distance, corridor width, shortlist size.
  • Structural. Alternative rule formulations and portfolio grouping logics.
  • Scale. Reach versus subcatchment and local versus aggregated prioritisation.

Verification interface and community translation

The verification layer is deliberately bounded. It does not try to become a full monitoring programme. It specifies what minimal follow-up is needed for credible implementation, and it can distinguish formal ecological assessment from citizen-facing stewardship tools.

Formal assessment

Q-value remains the formal ecological assessment anchor for river condition where expert-led biological classification is required.

Citizen-facing screening

CSSI and SSIS can sit in the community-facing verification interface as stewardship and adaptive-review tools rather than direct substitutes for regulatory assessment.

Nanny Watch

Monthly sampling at fixed points per subcatchment creates a social-scientific loop between community observations, management adaptation, and public accountability.

Trust translation role

The River Trust and Sonairte provide a governance and community interface that keeps the outputs inspectable and usable beyond academia.

Minimum-viable verification

The SSDS specifies indicators, directional progress, triggers for review, and what minimum information is required before action is treated as credible.

Outputs, risks, and what the user sees

Every major stage produces an inspectable artefact. That is part of the feasibility story. The site highlights the deliverables that make the SSDS legible to technical and non-technical audiences alike.

Scientific outputs

  • Methods paper on stability-aware SSDS design.
  • Demonstrator paper for Nanny-Delvin.
  • Thesis contribution on portfolios and scaling.
  • Data paper or methods appendix on reach-audit validation.

Practical outputs

  • Priority atlas and stability maps.
  • Portfolio-specific verification templates.
  • Rule sheet, assumptions register, and decision log.
  • Rebuild instructions for transfer to other catchments.

Risk controls

  • Versioned rule sheet to manage contestation.
  • Conservative classification where data quality is weak.
  • Explicit scope boundary against process-response sprawl.
  • Bounded field component tied to validation, not monitoring expansion.
